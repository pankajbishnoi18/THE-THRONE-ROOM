# THE TIMELINE OF AETHERIS

> *"A nation that remembers only its victories understands neither its failures nor its future. The duty of history is not to praise the past, but to preserve it."*
>
> — High Archivist Albrecht Corwin, Opening Address to the Royal Historical Society

---

# Introduction

Every generation inherits two legacies from those who came before it. The first is the physical world they leave behind: the cities they built, the roads they paved, the fortresses they defended, and the fields they cultivated. The second, often less visible but no less important, is the accumulated memory of their successes, failures, ambitions, and mistakes. Together these memories become history, allowing later generations to understand not only what happened, but why the world exists in its present form.

No institution within Aetheris values this responsibility more highly than the Royal Archives. Every treaty signed beneath the authority of the High Crown, every royal decree issued by successive monarchs, every census, engineering survey, diplomatic agreement, military campaign, and provincial record ultimately becomes part of a historical collection whose purpose extends far beyond simple preservation. The Archives exist because experience has repeatedly demonstrated that civilizations unable to remember their past eventually find themselves repeating it.

The history of Aetheris spans nearly fifteen centuries of recorded civilization. During that time, isolated settlements grew into villages, villages expanded into prosperous cities, and competing regional powers gradually evolved into the Seven Kingdoms recognized today. Dynasties rose and disappeared, trade routes shifted, religious traditions spread across the continent, and countless ordinary citizens contributed to developments that later generations would simply describe as history.

It is important to understand that Aetherian history is not remembered as an uninterrupted sequence of wars or royal successions. Although battles and monarchs occupy an important place within official records, historians generally divide the past according to broader changes in civilization itself. Certain periods witnessed rapid expansion as new settlements transformed wilderness into farmland. Others emphasized scholarship, engineering, commerce, or constitutional reform. Some generations experienced devastating conflict, while others enjoyed decades of remarkable peace during which institutions developed quietly without attracting the attention usually reserved for military victories.

This method of studying history reflects the values of the continent itself. Governments change, rulers die, and borders occasionally shift, but the gradual evolution of civilization continues across generations. A bridge may outlive the king who commissioned it. A university may educate scholars centuries after its founders are forgotten. A law established during one dynasty may continue protecting citizens long after the banners of that dynasty have vanished from public squares. Consequently, historians within Aetheris concern themselves not merely with political events but with the long-term development of the institutions that shaped everyday life.

The chronology presented in this volume therefore serves two purposes. It provides an ordered account of the major historical eras recognized by the Royal Archives while also explaining how each period contributed to the civilization inherited by the modern age. Rather than treating history as a disconnected collection of dates, these records seek to demonstrate the relationships between one generation and the next. Every era emerged from circumstances created by those preceding it, and every decision left consequences inherited by those who followed.

Modern citizens often regard the constitutional stability of Aetheris as something almost inevitable. The historian knows otherwise. Peace was built gradually through centuries of experimentation, hardship, compromise, and sacrifice. The institutions governing the continent today were not designed all at once but refined across hundreds of years by rulers, scholars, engineers, merchants, soldiers, and ordinary citizens whose names rarely appear within the grand narratives of history.

To study the past is therefore to understand the present.

Only by tracing the long path from scattered settlements to a united continent can one fully appreciate the civilization that exists today.

# Before the Royal Calendar

Long before official records measured the passing of years, the continent that would one day become Aetheris was a land of immense natural diversity inhabited by scattered tribal communities, seasonal hunters, river fishermen, and small agricultural settlements whose histories survive today only through archaeological discoveries, oral traditions, and fragments preserved within later chronicles.

Little is known with certainty about these earliest inhabitants. They left behind no centralized government, no standardized writing system, and few permanent monuments capable of surviving the passage of centuries. Instead, evidence of their existence emerges gradually through abandoned settlements discovered beside ancient rivers, burial sites overlooking mountain valleys, weathered stone circles hidden deep within forests, and primitive farming tools recovered from beneath layers of earth accumulated over countless generations.

Modern scholars generally believe that these early communities developed independently according to the environments surrounding them. River valleys supported the first successful agriculture, allowing populations to settle permanently rather than follow seasonal migration. Forest communities depended upon hunting, gathering, and woodland resources, while coastal settlements combined fishing with limited maritime trade along nearby shores. Mountain populations adapted to harsher climates by constructing fortified villages capable of surviving severe winters and protecting valuable mineral deposits.

Although separated by geography, these communities were never entirely isolated. Archaeological excavations reveal evidence of trade long before organized kingdoms appeared. Decorative stone unavailable within certain regions has been discovered hundreds of miles from its geological source. Seashells originating along the eastern coastline have been recovered from inland burial sites, while early copper tools demonstrate that knowledge of metallurgy spread gradually between otherwise distant settlements. Such discoveries suggest that even the earliest inhabitants recognized the practical advantages of exchanging goods and ideas beyond their immediate surroundings.

The absence of written records makes it impossible to reconstruct this era with complete certainty. Much of what is accepted today remains informed interpretation rather than established fact. Nevertheless, historians generally agree upon one important conclusion. The foundations of later civilization were laid not by conquering empires arriving from elsewhere, but by generations of local communities slowly adapting to the continent they inhabited. Agriculture, trade, craftsmanship, and regional cooperation all emerged long before the appearance of kings or formal governments.

For this reason, the Royal Archives describe the period preceding the first permanent settlements not as an age of darkness or ignorance, but as the formative era during which the relationship between people and the land was first established. Every civilization inherits a landscape shaped by nature. The earliest inhabitants of Aetheris were the first to learn how that landscape could sustain human society.

Their names have largely been forgotten.

Their influence has not.

# The Age of Settlement Begins

The beginning of recorded history is traditionally marked by the establishment of the first permanent settlement upon the fertile banks of the Silverflow River, an event from which the Royal Calendar derives its dating system. Although smaller communities undoubtedly existed beforehand, this settlement represented something fundamentally different. It endured across generations, developed organized leadership, established regular agriculture, and gradually became the nucleus around which surrounding villages would eventually grow.

Historians identify this moment not because it was the largest settlement of its time, but because it introduced a new understanding of permanence. Earlier communities often relocated according to changing seasons or environmental conditions. The settlers along the Silverflow instead invested in irrigation, durable buildings, grain storage, defensive earthworks, and communal institutions intended to serve not merely themselves but their descendants.

This decision altered the course of continental history.

Permanent agriculture produced reliable food surpluses capable of supporting craftsmen, builders, traders, and administrators whose labor no longer depended directly upon farming. Population increased steadily, encouraging specialization within the growing settlement. Roads appeared where repeated travel connected neighboring communities. Markets developed as surplus goods were exchanged rather than consumed locally. Leadership evolved from temporary necessity into recognizable systems of governance responsible for organizing labor, resolving disputes, and defending the settlement against external threats.

Many customs still observed throughout modern Aetheris trace their origins to this period. Seasonal harvest festivals began as communal celebrations marking successful food production. Public granaries emerged from the practical necessity of surviving poor harvests. Shared labor during construction projects established traditions of civic responsibility that later generations would elevate into moral virtue. Even the earliest forms of written accounting developed not for scholarship but for recording grain supplies, livestock ownership, and agreements between neighboring communities.

The people living during the Age of Settlement could scarcely have imagined the kingdoms, cities, universities, and constitutional institutions that would eventually arise from their efforts. Yet the principles they established—cooperation, long-term planning, stewardship of the land, and investment in future generations—continued shaping Aetherian civilization long after their own settlements disappeared beneath expanding towns and changing landscapes.

For this reason, the founding of the first permanent settlement remains more than the beginning of the calendar.

It marks the moment when scattered communities first began the long journey toward becoming a civilization.

# The Age of Kingdoms

As permanent settlements multiplied across the continent, differences in geography gradually produced differences in society. Communities separated by mountains developed unlike those established upon fertile plains. Coastal towns learned to navigate changing tides, while inland villages depended upon rivers and seasonal harvests. Forest settlements became skilled hunters, herbalists, and woodworkers, whereas mineral-rich highlands encouraged mining and metalworking. Over generations these regional distinctions became increasingly pronounced, eventually giving rise to larger political communities whose identities extended far beyond individual settlements.

The emergence of the first kingdoms did not occur through a single dramatic event. Rather, it was the gradual consequence of population growth, economic specialization, and the practical necessity of organized government. As settlements expanded into towns and towns into cities, local leaders found themselves responsible for increasingly complex societies. Roads required maintenance, disputes demanded impartial judgment, food surpluses had to be protected, and distant villages sought common leadership during periods of external danger. Authority that had once existed only within isolated communities slowly expanded across entire regions.

These early kingdoms differed considerably from their modern descendants. Their borders remained fluid, changing with every generation as alliances formed and dissolved. Many rulers possessed authority only so long as they maintained the loyalty of neighboring settlements, and succession disputes frequently divided territories that had taken decades to unite. Political institutions were still primitive, relying heavily upon personal loyalty rather than established systems of law. The strength of a kingdom depended largely upon the wisdom of its ruler, making periods of prosperity vulnerable to abrupt decline whenever capable leadership gave way to incompetence or civil conflict.

Despite these uncertainties, the Age of Kingdoms produced remarkable achievements. Organized agriculture expanded across fertile plains, allowing populations to increase at an unprecedented rate. Permanent roads connected neighboring towns, facilitating trade that reached farther than ever before. Defensive fortifications evolved from simple wooden palisades into formidable stone castles overlooking strategic rivers, mountain passes, and trade routes. Craftsmen established workshops whose techniques gradually spread between regions, while merchants carried not only goods but also ideas, customs, and technological innovations from one kingdom to another.

The development of written administration proved equally transformative. Earlier settlements had relied primarily upon oral tradition, yet growing kingdoms required more reliable methods of preserving agreements, taxation records, legal judgments, and property ownership. Scribes emerged as indispensable officials whose responsibilities extended far beyond simple record keeping. Through their work, governments acquired memory. Decisions no longer disappeared with the deaths of those who made them but remained available for future generations to consult, revise, or challenge.

Religion also assumed a more organized role during this period. Local traditions that had once differed from village to village gradually developed into broader systems of belief shared across entire regions. Temples became centers not only of worship but of education, charity, medicine, and scholarship. Many early rulers strengthened their legitimacy by supporting religious institutions, while priests often served as advisers, historians, and diplomats whose influence extended beyond political boundaries.

Yet perhaps the defining characteristic of the Age of Kingdoms was not stability but competition.

Every successful harvest encouraged population growth, and every growing population demanded additional resources. Fertile valleys, navigable rivers, strategic mountain passes, and prosperous trade routes became objects of increasing political importance. Kingdoms that had once interacted only occasionally now found themselves sharing borders, competing for influence, and negotiating relationships whose consequences extended far beyond local concerns.

Some disputes were resolved through marriage alliances, commercial agreements, or carefully negotiated compromises.

Others were settled upon the battlefield.

Throughout these centuries, warfare remained a recurring feature of continental life. Few conflicts sought complete conquest. Most centered upon control of specific territories, succession disputes, trade privileges, or competing interpretations of ancient agreements. Victorious kingdoms often gained temporary advantages, yet these successes rarely proved permanent. Defeated rivals recovered, new alliances emerged, and future generations resumed contests their ancestors believed had already been resolved.

Historians frequently describe this era as one of continuous motion. Borders shifted, dynasties rose and fell, cities prospered or declined, and political influence flowed between regions with remarkable speed. Although exhausting for those who lived through it, this competition also accelerated innovation. Better roads, stronger fortifications, improved agricultural methods, more efficient administration, and increasingly sophisticated diplomacy all developed during centuries when survival often depended upon adaptation.

By the closing centuries of the Age of Kingdoms, seven major regional powers had gradually distinguished themselves from countless lesser realms. Each possessed established capitals, recognizable dynasties, organized administrations, and growing influence over surrounding territories. They were not yet the Seven Kingdoms recognized by the modern constitution, but they represented the foundations from which those future kingdoms would emerge.

The stage had been prepared for the next great transformation in continental history.

# The Age of Expansion

Following centuries of regional consolidation, Aetheris entered a period historians commonly describe as the Age of Expansion. Unlike the earlier centuries, during which kingdoms concentrated primarily upon establishing internal stability, rulers increasingly turned their attention toward development beyond their existing centers of power. New settlements appeared along rivers, forests were gradually cleared for agriculture, mountain passes once considered impassable became important commercial corridors, and maritime exploration extended trade beyond the familiar coastline.

Population growth provided much of the momentum behind this transformation.

Improved farming techniques, more reliable irrigation systems, and greater political stability enabled communities to support larger populations than ever before. Growing towns demanded additional farmland, craftsmen required increased supplies of raw materials, and merchants sought access to markets capable of sustaining expanding commercial networks. Expansion therefore became not merely an expression of political ambition but an economic necessity.

Road construction accelerated dramatically during this era. Earlier generations had built routes connecting neighboring settlements, but the expanding kingdoms required infrastructure capable of supporting long-distance trade on an unprecedented scale. Stone bridges replaced temporary crossings, mountain roads were carved into previously inaccessible terrain, and caravan stations provided shelter for travelers journeying between distant regions. These projects demanded enormous investments of labor and resources, yet their benefits quickly became apparent. Goods that once required months to transport could now reach distant markets in a fraction of the time, encouraging specialization and strengthening regional economies.

Commerce flourished alongside infrastructure. Merchant families established permanent trading houses in major cities, while guilds emerged to regulate standards of quality, apprenticeship, and professional conduct. River traffic expanded as improved vessels carried heavier cargoes between inland settlements. Coastal ports welcomed ships whose captains ventured farther into the Sapphire Sea with every generation, gradually introducing foreign goods, unfamiliar ideas, and new opportunities for prosperity.

The expansion of trade produced profound cultural consequences. Travelers encountered customs unlike those of their own homelands, scholars exchanged knowledge across political borders, and artisans adapted techniques learned from distant regions. Although each kingdom retained its unique identity, the movement of people and ideas fostered a growing awareness that they belonged to a broader continental civilization rather than entirely isolated societies.

Cities likewise entered a period of remarkable growth. Administrative centers evolved into thriving urban communities whose populations supported universities, temples, marketplaces, workshops, and public institutions unimaginable only a few generations earlier. Urban life encouraged increasingly specialized professions. Architects designed larger public buildings, physicians accumulated medical knowledge through wider experience, and legal scholars refined systems of governance capable of managing populations far larger than those known during earlier centuries.

The relationship between rulers and their subjects also changed during this period. Earlier monarchs had derived much of their authority from military leadership and personal loyalty. As governments became more sophisticated, successful administration depended increasingly upon officials, judges, engineers, tax collectors, and scholars capable of managing complex societies. Governance gradually evolved from personal rule toward institutional administration, a transformation whose significance would become fully apparent only in later centuries.

Not every consequence of expansion proved beneficial.

Prosperity heightened competition between kingdoms whose commercial interests increasingly overlapped. Wealth generated through trade funded larger armies, stronger fortifications, and ambitious construction projects intended to demonstrate political prestige. Merchant rivalries occasionally spilled into diplomatic disputes, while control of strategic roads, ports, and rivers acquired greater importance than ever before. Expansion therefore strengthened civilization while simultaneously creating new sources of tension among its most successful kingdoms.

Historians often describe this contradiction as one of the defining features of the age. The same roads that carried merchants also carried armies. The same prosperity that financed universities funded military campaigns. The same political stability that encouraged commerce gave rulers the resources necessary to pursue greater influence.

Beneath the appearance of flourishing civilization, rival ambitions continued to grow.

The kingdoms had become wealthier, stronger, and more interconnected than any previous generation could have imagined.

They had also become increasingly capable of waging conflicts whose consequences would affect the entire continent.

The achievements of the Age of Expansion laid the foundations for the greatest prosperity yet witnessed in Aetherian history.

They also created the conditions from which its greatest crisis would eventually emerge.

# The Age of Unification

By the closing years of the Age of Expansion, the continent had reached a level of prosperity unprecedented in its history. Roads connected distant capitals, merchant caravans crossed hundreds of miles without leaving civilized territory, universities exchanged scholars between kingdoms, and cities grew larger than any previous generation had imagined possible. To many contemporaries it appeared that civilization had reached a permanent golden age.

History would prove otherwise.

Prosperity had strengthened every kingdom, but it had also magnified their ambitions. Trade routes that once served purely economic purposes acquired immense strategic importance. Mountain passes controlled access to valuable mineral resources. River crossings determined commercial dominance. Coastal ports became gateways through which enormous fortunes flowed. As wealth accumulated, political influence became increasingly intertwined with economic power, and competition between neighboring kingdoms grew sharper with every passing decade.

Unlike the localized conflicts of earlier centuries, disputes now possessed continental consequences. A disagreement between two kingdoms could interrupt trade across half the continent. Merchant guilds found themselves financing rival governments. Neutral provinces struggled to preserve commerce while armies maneuvered along major highways. Diplomats worked tirelessly to prevent crises from escalating, yet many of the institutions required to manage disputes on such a scale had not yet been created.

The kingdoms had outgrown the political systems inherited from earlier ages.

Unfortunately, few recognized this reality before events overtook them.

Several decades of increasing diplomatic tension culminated in what later historians would name **the War of Seven Banners**, the single most transformative conflict in the recorded history of Aetheris. Although no contemporary expected the war to define an entire era, it ultimately reshaped every aspect of continental civilization. Unlike previous wars fought over individual provinces or disputed succession, this conflict drew every major kingdom into a struggle whose effects extended far beyond the battlefield.

The causes of the war were numerous rather than singular. Long-standing territorial disagreements combined with commercial rivalry, competing military alliances, dynastic uncertainty, and mutual distrust accumulated over generations. Each kingdom believed its actions justified by circumstances, while each simultaneously regarded the actions of its rivals as unacceptable threats to regional stability. Diplomacy gradually collapsed beneath the weight of accumulated grievances until armed conflict appeared, at least to those living through it, unavoidable.

The war lasted for years.

Its consequences endured for centuries.

Entire regions that had flourished during the Age of Expansion suffered catastrophic disruption. Agricultural production collapsed wherever armies repeatedly crossed cultivated land. Trade routes that once carried prosperity instead transported soldiers, refugees, and military supplies. Cities spent fortunes constructing defensive fortifications while neglecting infrastructure that had supported generations of peaceful commerce. Families found themselves divided as successive campaigns demanded increasing numbers of recruits from every kingdom.

Perhaps the greatest tragedy lay not in military defeat, but in the growing realization that victory itself offered no meaningful solution. Even successful campaigns imposed enormous economic costs. Occupied territories required permanent garrisons. Conquered populations resisted foreign administration. Merchants abandoned unstable regions regardless of which banner flew above local castles. Every apparent triumph created new obligations while solving remarkably few of the underlying political problems.

By the conflict's final years, rulers across the continent confronted an uncomfortable truth.

No kingdom could win without ensuring that every kingdom ultimately lost.

The war marked the end of an entire political philosophy.

For centuries rulers had believed that security could be achieved through military superiority alone. The devastation of the War of Seven Banners demonstrated instead that the prosperity of one kingdom had become inseparable from the prosperity of its neighbors. The continent had grown too interconnected for perpetual rivalry to remain sustainable.

This realization would become the foundation upon which modern Aetheris was built.

# The Treaty of Seven Crowns

When the final campaigns ended, the rulers of the Seven Kingdoms faced a decision unlike any confronted by their predecessors. They could negotiate another temporary peace, redraw borders once again, and leave future generations to repeat the same cycle of rivalry that had defined previous centuries.

Or they could attempt something entirely unprecedented.

The negotiations that followed remain among the most studied political events in Aetherian history. Representatives from every kingdom gathered not merely to conclude a war but to reconsider the principles upon which continental government itself should rest. Months of debate produced countless disagreements regarding sovereignty, military authority, taxation, diplomacy, succession, and constitutional law. Yet beneath these disagreements existed one point upon which every delegation ultimately agreed.

The old system had failed.

From this shared conclusion emerged the **Treaty of Seven Crowns**.

Rather than abolishing the kingdoms, the treaty formally recognized their continued sovereignty while establishing institutions responsible for matters affecting the continent as a whole. Every kingdom retained its monarch, regional government, legal traditions, and cultural identity. At the same time, they accepted that diplomacy, continental defence, constitutional arbitration, and shared infrastructure could no longer remain entirely independent responsibilities.

The creation of the High Crown represented the treaty's most significant innovation, though contemporary observers often misunderstood its purpose. It was never intended to replace the kings and queens of the Seven Kingdoms, nor to establish an empire governed from a single throne. Instead, the High Crown became the constitutional guardian of interests shared by every kingdom simultaneously. Its legitimacy rested upon mutual agreement rather than military conquest, making it unlike any monarchy previously known upon the continent.

Equally important was the change in political philosophy accompanying the treaty.

For the first time in recorded history, peace ceased to be viewed merely as the absence of war. Instead, it became an active responsibility requiring institutions capable of resolving disagreements before they escalated into armed conflict. Diplomacy acquired unprecedented importance. Written constitutional law assumed authority beyond individual rulers. Shared infrastructure became a continental obligation rather than a regional luxury. Governments increasingly measured success not by territorial expansion but by the stability and prosperity they provided to their citizens.

Historians frequently describe the Treaty of Seven Crowns as the moment Aetheris ceased to be simply a collection of neighboring kingdoms and became a civilization united by common institutions.

Its greatest achievement was not ending one war.

It was making future wars far less likely.

# The Age of Reconstruction

Peace alone could not erase the consequences of generations spent in conflict.

The years immediately following the Treaty of Seven Crowns demanded an effort unlike any previously undertaken. Roads destroyed during military campaigns required rebuilding. Bridges had to reconnect regions isolated by war. Irrigation systems neglected during decades of conflict needed restoration before agriculture could fully recover. Cities welcomed refugees returning to homes that no longer existed, while governments struggled to restore public confidence in institutions still finding their place within the new constitutional order.

The work of reconstruction extended far beyond physical rebuilding.

Equally important was the restoration of trust.

Former enemies now sat together within newly established councils. Merchant guilds resumed trade across borders that only recently separated hostile armies. Universities reopened exchanges of scholars whose education had been interrupted by war. Engineers, physicians, and administrators travelled between kingdoms, sharing expertise without regard for former military alliances.

Many of the institutions regarded as ordinary today originated during this remarkable period. Continental standards governing commerce, infrastructure, education, and administration gradually emerged because reconstruction required cooperation on a scale impossible for individual kingdoms to achieve alone. The Royal Archives expanded rapidly as governments recognized the importance of preserving accurate records for future generations. Public investment increasingly favored projects benefiting the continent collectively rather than individual rulers alone.

Reconstruction demanded patience.

Entire generations devoted their lives to rebuilding a civilization they knew they might never see fully completed.

Their efforts succeeded beyond expectation.

Within little more than a century, Aetheris entered one of the most prosperous periods in its recorded history.

---

# The Modern Era

The current year, 1483 After Settlement, belongs to what historians commonly describe as the Modern Era, a period characterized less by conquest than by administration, commerce, scholarship, and constitutional development.

The Seven Kingdoms continue governing themselves while cooperating through institutions established after the Treaty of Seven Crowns. Merchant caravans travel safely along Royal Highways linking every major city. Universities educate students from across the continent. Maritime trade extends beyond the Sapphire Sea, introducing new goods and unfamiliar cultures. Advances in engineering, agriculture, medicine, and administration steadily improve the quality of life enjoyed by ordinary citizens.

Yet history has not ended.

The challenges confronting modern Aetheris differ from those faced by earlier generations, but they remain no less significant. Rapid urban growth places increasing pressure upon infrastructure. Merchant guilds possess economic influence unimaginable to their predecessors. Expanding trade introduces both opportunity and political complexity. Provincial governments administer populations larger than many ancient kingdoms, while foreign powers observe the continent with growing interest.

The constitutional order continues adapting to circumstances its founders could never have anticipated.

This capacity for peaceful adaptation may ultimately become its greatest strength.

---

# Conclusion

The history of Aetheris is often summarized through battles, treaties, royal successions, and the rise or fall of kingdoms. Such events undoubtedly shaped the course of civilization, yet they represent only the visible moments within a much longer story.

The true history of the continent is the gradual accumulation of knowledge across generations.

It is found in roads that continued serving travelers centuries after the engineers who designed them had died. It survives within laws refined by countless judges, universities sustained by generations of scholars, and institutions strengthened through repeated experience rather than sudden inspiration. Every generation inherited a world imperfectly understood, improved some part of it through labor and sacrifice, and entrusted those improvements to the future.

Modern Aetheris exists because countless individuals believed they were building something that would outlive them.

Their names fill the shelves of the Royal Archives.

Their legacy is the civilization itself.

The study of history therefore serves not merely to preserve memory, but to remind every generation that the future will one day judge them as they now judge those who came before.
